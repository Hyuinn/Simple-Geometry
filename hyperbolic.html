Hyperbolic Geometry

In Hyperbolic Geometry, there are two commonly used models: the Hyperbolic Plane and the Hyperbolic Disk. We will talk briefly about both of them but we will need some results from Möbius Transformations that we won't cover in details.

One of such results is that those transformations build an Isomorphism between the Hyperbolic Plane and the Hyperbolic Disk. To put it simply, an Isomorphism will preserve shapes and distances, so every result we achieve on one of them will be carried to the other as long as we're using Möbius Transformations to move from one to the other.

Hyperbolic Plane

To construct the Hyperbolic Plane we begin by describing points and lines without any reference to distance.
Lets take the upper half-plane \( H = \{x + iy \text{ }; y > 0\} \) in \( \mathbb{C} \) as our Hyperbolic Plane.
Notice that the \(x\)-axis (horizontal axis) is NOT part of \(H\).
A Hyperbolic Line will be a semicircle in \(H\) whose center lies in \( \mathbb{R} \) and are orthogonal to \( \mathbb{R} \).
However we must also regard 'straight lines' that are perpendicular to \( \mathbb{R} \) as our hyperbolic lines.

Since the horizontal axis is not part of our set, that means those semi-circles and lines in the image above don't intersect each other within our set, and therefore they are all parallel to one another.
Unlike Elliptical Geometry, this breaks the Fifth Postulate by having too many Parallels (infinite in fact) instead of just one!

Möbius Transformation

A Möbius Transformation or Möbius Map is a function \(f\) of a complex variable that can be written in the form
\( f(z) = \displaystyle \frac{(az+b)}{(cz+d)} \) , where \( (ad-bc) \ne 0 \)

Without getting into too many details about those, it suffices for us to know that this particular family of functions have the property of mapping Lines into Lines or Circles and mapping Circles into Circles or Lines.
Knowing that, we can conclude that Möbius Transformation maps Hyperbolic Lines into Hyperbolic Lines.

Hyperbolic Disk

Our second Hyperbolic model is the unitary disk \( D = \{z \in \mathbb{C}; |z| < 1\} \).

Consider the following Möbius Transformation \( g(z) = \displaystyle \frac{(z-i)}{(z+i)} \).
It maps \(H\) into \(D\), which means that our results on the plane \(H\) are carried over to the disk \(D\).

The idea is that we somehow glued both ends of the \(x\)-axis from the \(H\) plane together
This model (as any Hyperbolic one) also has infinite parallels to a given line that cross the same point that's outside of it.
